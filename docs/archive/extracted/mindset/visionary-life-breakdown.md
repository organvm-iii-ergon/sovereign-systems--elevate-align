Such a great question—and yes, there’s actually a lot of gentle psychology and nervous system insight behind this choice. Let’s break it down both ways so you can feel into what’s best for you right now:



📞 Calling Someone While Eating Dinner


Pros:


Mimics the village: Humans evolved eating together. Sharing meals releases oxytocin (the connection hormone), which calms your nervous system and makes digestion smoother.

Can help with loneliness: If you’ve been craving connection, a sweet call with someone you love can feel nourishing emotionally and physically.

Keeps you present: If it’s a real, intentional convo (not just scrolling while talking), it pulls you out of autopilot.


Watch for:
If the person’s energy is draining or chaotic, it can raise cortisol and inhibit digestion. Choose calming, uplifting people—this isn’t the time for drama-dumps.



📺 Watching TV While Eating


Pros:


Can feel comforting: Especially if you live alone or feel touched-out from the day, a familiar show can simulate a cozy “roommate” vibe.

Helps some neurodivergent folks eat more easily: For those who struggle with mealtime focus or overwhelm, gentle distraction can make eating feel safer or easier.


Cons:


Mindless eating: You’re less likely to chew slowly, savor your food, or stop when you’re full.

Disconnects you from fullness cues: Digestion begins with awareness—sight, smell, taste. TV can override this.

Over time, can reinforce emotional numbing instead of emotional nourishment.



🌿 Third Option: Eating as Ritual


Light a candle, put on soft music or a podcast, and sit with your food like it's sacred. This is amazing for hormone regulation, blood sugar balance, and rewiring your relationship with self.



✨ My Suggestion?


Let it depend on the need:


If you’re feeling lonely → call someone safe, even just to check in. A 10-minute chat can light up your heart.

If you’re overstimulated → skip the TV, play calming music, and just be with yourself and your food.

If you’re mentally burnt out → maybe a comfort show is okay—but try to stay aware of your bites and chew mindfully between laughs.
