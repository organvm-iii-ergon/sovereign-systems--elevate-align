## Response:
You’ve asked a number of claims, so I’ll go through them one by one and indicate what is true, what is unsupported, and what remains unclear or exaggerated.



✅ What is supported by evidence


It is well-documented that women (and female bodies) are under-represented in many areas of medical research.
For example, an analysis of 1,433 clinical trials (2016-2019) found only about 41.2% of participants were female. Harvard Medical School+2PMC+2

Earlier work noted that in many fields female representation in early-phase trials is especially low (e.g., 29-34% in certain industry-sponsored early-phase trials). Clinical Trials Arena+1

Experts describe a “gender health data gap”: one part is that diseases disproportionately affecting women have less data, and another is that male physiology has often been treated as the default. PMC+2AAMC+2

The historical exploitation of enslaved Black women in the development of gynecology is also well-documented.
J. Marion Sims (1813-1883) developed surgical approaches for vesicovaginal fistula by operating (often without anesthesia) on enslaved Black women (e.g., Anarcha, Lucy, Betsy) in Alabama. HISTORY+3AAIHS+3PMC+3

These events are part of the larger history of medical and research exploitation of Black women’s bodies in the U.S. and colonial contexts. Equal Justice Initiative+1

Research on pregnant women, and women of reproductive age, has been historically excluded or under‐prioritised in many clinical trials.
For example, guidelines from the National Institutes of Health (NIH) now emphasise including women and minorities in research but this is a relatively recent evolution. ORWH+1



❓ What is not clearly supported (or is an exaggeration)


The claim “only 5% of western medicine studies has been on the female body” — I could not find credible evidence to support a blanket figure of “5%”.
The data I found shows that women’s participation in trials is low in some areas, but not generally only 5%. For example, female participation around 41% in trials. Harvard Medical School+1

Another figure says women’s health (products/services) accounts for about “4% of research & development funding” in healthcare products — that’s funding, not “studies on the female body.” Oliver Wyman

Therefore, the “5%” figure appears to be an oversimplification or mis-statement of some funding/statistics but not accurate as stated.

The breakdown “3% breast cancer and 2% pregnancy/uterine issues/puberty/menopause” — I found no credible data that supports such precise percentages of “studies” being divided in that manner.
It’s unclear where the “3% breast cancer” and “2% other female reproductive issues” numbers come from.

So this specific claim appears not substantiated by peer-reviewed research that I located.

The claim that “most of that 2% was done on slaves non-consensually” — while the historical exploitation of enslaved women in gynecology is true, the claim that most modern studies on pregnancy/uterine/puberty/menopause areas were done on enslaved women is not supported.
The historical work (e.g., Sims) was in the 19th century; modern studies are overwhelmingly not on enslaved persons.

So, this is a conflation of some real historical unethical research with a sweeping claim that is incorrect in the modern context.

Regarding the claim that “how they test for cervical cancer is with a whole punch — they believe women have a higher pain tolerance and most of the tools used are harsh sharp cold and there’s no warning about the scraping cutting or hole punching down there” — this mixes multiple assertions:
Some cervical screening takes a “punch biopsy” or an excisional biopsy (cone biopsy) in certain cases of abnormal results, which is invasive.

But the claim that the reason is “they believe women have a higher pain tolerance” is not supported by mainstream legitimate sources. I found no good evidence that current cervical screening tools are used because of a belief that women tolerate more pain.

The statement “there’s no warning about the scraping/cutting…” is a generalisation — in many healthcare systems, informed consent is required and patients are told about the procedure and potential discomfort, though patient experiences vary widely and many patients report being inadequately prepared.

In short: there are invasive tools in gynecology (biopsies etc), and there is a history of tools and approaches that were developed in exploitative contexts, but the specific claim as a whole is not accurate as a description of current practice or consensus.

It is true that women are under-represented in many medical research studies and that there is a legacy of unethical medical practices involving enslaved women.

It is not accurate to claim that only 5% of all Western medicine studies have been on women’s bodies — the data do not support such a low figure.

The breakdown into “3% breast cancer / 2% other female reproductive issues” is unsupported.

The claim that “most of that small share was done on non-consenting slaves” is a mis-characterisation when applied to all modern research — yes slavery era research was unethical and exploited women, but modern research is not based on that.

Some of the concerns about gynecological tools and patient experience are valid (e.g., invasive procedures, inadequate communication), but the rationale “women have higher pain tolerance” is not supported by credible current evidence, and the claim is overly broad.

## Response:

I’ll break this down into three parts: what we do know about birth control approval & side-effects, what we know about its relationship with conditions like Endometriosis (and what’s not clear), and how the concept of “fascia” (and the body as a mechanical/structural system) might tie in with all of this.



✅ What we do know about birth control


Yes — there are many birth-control methods that are approved by the U.S. Food & Drug Administration (FDA).
The FDA lists “FDA-Approved Methods of Birth Control” (oral contraceptives, IUDs, implants, shots) in their guidance. U.S. Food and Drug Administration+2teenpregnancy.dph.ncdhhs.gov+2

For example, the pill was first approved in the U.S. in 1960. HISTORY+1

More recently, the FDA approved the first over-the-counter daily oral contraceptive pill (Opill, containing norgestrel) in July 2023. U.S. Food and Drug Administration+1
So, the claim “birth control is not FDA approved” is incorrect in the general sense. Many methods are approved.

Yes — these methods often carry a “laundry list” of potential side-effects, and the way side-effects are communicated to patients can be sub-optimal.
For instance, hormonal birth control is indicated for use in conditions like endometriosis, but the side-effects listed include irregular bleeding, headache, nausea, breast tenderness, mood changes, weight changes, etc. Sydney Endometriosis+2Rachael Haverland Site+2

In one article: “Potential side effects include irregular bleeding, headaches, nausea, bloating, breast tenderness, mood changes, weight gain, and decreased libido.” Sydney Endometriosis

The way these side-effects are framed in clinical practice or during prescribing may often feel to patients like “we’re not telling you everything, or the experience is different than what you were prepared for.”
So your point that side-effects are real and sometimes under-communicated is absolutely valid.

Yes — in the context of endometriosis, hormonal birth control is used, but it’s not a universal “fix”.
For example: Hormonal birth control may help with endometriosis symptoms by suppressing ovulation, reducing the cyclical growth of endometrial-like tissue, and thereby reducing pain/bleeding. Pelvic Rehabilitation Medicine

But there are important caveats: it doesn’t eliminate lesions or adhesions, it doesn’t work for everyone (some women are “non-responders”), and in some cases it may worsen certain symptoms. Medical News Today+1

For example, one review found that around one-third of people with endometriosis do not respond adequately to hormonal birth control. Medical News Today+1
So your linking of birth control → endometriosis issues is on target: yes, there is a link (and complex one) and the experience can be very varied.



⚠️ What is less clear / what I want to flag as caveats


Approval doesn’t mean “perfect fit for everyone” or “risk-free”.
Even though methods are FDA-approved, that approval is typically for efficacy in preventing pregnancy and reasonable safety in the studied population. It does not guarantee that every patient will not experience significant side-effects, or that the tool will suit their unique body/biomechanics/health history.

Also the studied populations in trials often exclude pregnant women, some disease states, or certain age groups, which means real-world outcomes may differ.

Side-effects and consent may vary a lot in practice.
You mentioned “a laundry list of side-effects that are not talked about beforehand”. That definitely happens. Sometimes the full experience (e.g., mood changes, libido changes, structural pain, pelvic floor issues) is not emphasized, or the way the body interacts with the hormones like in fascia or connective tissue is not part of the usual discussion.

The body’s structural system (connective tissue, fascia, biomechanics) is less represented in mainstream research in this context.
When we talk about “fascia” (connective tissue web, the “body’s wiring” in a sense) — how hormones, inflammation, structural load, scarring, cycles, pain interrelate — that’s a much less studied realm compared to “hormones → organ” models.

So your intuition that something deeper is happening (beyond “hormones → bleed less / pain less”) is very valid, but the evidence base is thinner.



🔍 How “fascia” and body/structural systems might play into this


Let’s unpack the “fascia” idea — what it is, how hormones & inflammation interact with it, and how that might relate to conditions like endometriosis or pelvic pain.


Fascia is the connective tissue network in the body that surrounds muscles, bones, organs, nerves — it’s a continuous web, not just isolated strips. It has mechanical, neurological, and biochemical roles.

Hormones (like estrogen, progesterone) influence tissue remodeling, elasticity, fluid content, inflammation. So as the hormonal milieu shifts (whether naturally or via contraceptive hormones), the fascia/connective tissue may respond (for better or worse).

In conditions such as endometriosis, pelvic adhesions, scar tissue, chronic pelvic pain — you get inflammation, mechanical distortion, changes in tissue tension, nerve sensitisation. Fascia is part of that mechanical/structural system.

If a birth control method suppresses ovulation, reduces bleeding/cycle, etc — that may reduce some of the cyclical inflammatory load; but it may also alter the mechanical loading (less regular relax/constrict, shifts in connective tissue hydration, changes in ligament/tissue tension) — meaning some people could feel structural or fascial changes (pain, tightness, ‘‘deep ache’’, fibrotic responses) which are less visible in hormone-only models.

Essentially: The body is not just a bag of organs and hormones — it’s a mechanical and fluid system. So when you change one part (hormonal input, cycle, bleeding pattern), you change the interplay of load, tension, tissue repair, sensory nerve activation, fascia tension, scar formation.

In the case of endometriosis: You’ve got abnormal tissue growth, often with adhesions (scar-like connections), which create a mechanical anchor between organs/ligaments/fascia → that can distort the fascia/web and cause chronic pain. Hormonal birth control may suppress new growth, lighten bleeding, reduce pain — but it doesn’t always fix the mechanical distortions (adhesions, altered fascia) which may continue to create pain/tension. Some of the literature supports this: “For women who said the birth control pill was not effective for their pain, they reported higher levels of pain and more often had muscle…” PMC

Also, the idea of “repression” (your term) may relate to how the body stores trauma, tension, chronic inflammation, and how the fascia “holds memory” (not in a mystical way, but in a biomechanical way—tension, altered sliding surfaces, nerve entrapment). So if hormonal changes aren’t addressing the tissue load/tension/inflammation, the structural system might still express pain or dysfunction.



🎯 What this means for someone in real life (your perspective)


If you’ve used hormonal birth control and you feel “something else is going on” (e.g., persistent pelvic pain, structural or fascial tension, sensations of “tightness”, movement-related pain, weird bleed/pain cycles) — you’re absolutely not wrong. The standard model may not cover the full story.

It might be helpful to broaden the conversation with your practitioner (or find one who does) to include: connective tissue/fascial health, pelvic floor / myofascial tension, mechanical load/vs structural changes from adhesions, nerve sensitisation, how your cycle/hormones interact with your body’s biomechanics.

You might also ask: “What about hormone-free or lower-hormone options?” “What about addressing adhesion/fascia work (physical therapy, pelvic floor PT, scar mobilization) along with hormonal management?” Because if you only treat the hormone side and ignore the structural side — you might still feel unrest.

Side-effects matter. If you were not warned or did not experience what you were told you would — it’s valid to ask for a review of your hormone regime and how it interacts with your unique body (including fascia/connective tissue).

The “hidden” piece is: your body, your history, your tissue context (past surgeries, trauma, childhood/posture/movement patterns, inflammatory history) all matter. So don’t take “one size” for hormonal birth control or pain-management.
