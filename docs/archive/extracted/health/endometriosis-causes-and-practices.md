## Response:
It’s totally understandable that you’re frustrated — Endometriosis is a painful, complex condition and the “standard” suggestions (hormones, surgery) often feel limiting. Let’s unpack what is known about causes and explore how some eastern-medicine perspectives and practices approach it. I’ll also offer caveats (very important) so you can evaluate what might feel useful for you.



What we do know about causes


First, a frank reminder: researchers do not have a definitive cause for endometriosis. NCBI+3World Health Organization+3Mount Sinai Health System+3
However, many factors are associated with or likely contributors. Here are key ones:


Biological / Western-medicine perspectives


One leading theory: during menstruation, some of the uterine lining flows “backwards” through the fallopian tubes (retrograde menstruation) and implants on pelvic organs. Mount Sinai Health System+2NCBI+2

Hormonal imbalance: The lining-like tissue outside the uterus still responds to estrogen (and is less inhibited by progesterone) so it proliferates, causes inflammation, scar tissue. NCBI+1

Immune system dysregulation: People with endometriosis tend to have altered immune responses, which may allow ectopic tissue to survive and cause inflammation. World Health Organization+1

Genetic/familial predisposition: Family history increases risk. Polytechnique Insights+1

Environmental/other factors (less established): Exposure to certain toxins, oxidative stress, etc. Wikipedia+1


Why it may be increasing (or at least more recognized)


You asked if it has “doubled in the last ten years” — I couldn’t find strong evidence for a doubling in incidence over exactly the last decade. However:


Awareness is much higher now, diagnostic methods have improved.

More people are being diagnosed earlier.

Environmental/lifestyle factors may be changing (though this is complex).
So the perceived increase may be a mix of true rise + better detection + more reporting.



Eastern-medicine / Traditional paradigms & how they interpret causes


Here’s where it gets interesting: Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) and other “eastern” systems frame things differently — not better or worse, just differently — often focusing on patterns of imbalance rather than a single “disease lesion”. These can provide additional tools/insights. If you’re open, they might add to (not necessarily replace) what you do.


TCM / Chinese Medicine viewpoint


According to TCM:


Endometriosis is often categorized under the notion of blood stasis (“blood stagnation”) and/or qi stagnation (blocked energy flow) in the pelvic area. Rebalance Traditional Chinese Medicine+2PMC+2

Other patterns:
“Liver qi stagnation” leading to blood stasis. The liver in TCM is related to flow, regulation. Rebalance Traditional Chinese Medicine+1

“Cold in the uterus” or “kidney yang deficiency” meaning the pelvic region is cold/undernourished and circulation is poor. Rebalance Traditional Chinese Medicine

“Dampness” or “phlegm/humor accumulation” affecting the reproductive organs via the spleen/qi. Rebalance Traditional Chinese Medicine

Based on this, the “cause” is seen as blocked circulation, stagnant energy (qi) or cold/damp in the pelvic region, which over time manifests as pain, growth of abnormal tissue, irregular flow. (Remember: in TCM, “cause” is more about the internal milieu than a microbial or structural lesion).


What eastern-practices do (and what evidence says)


Here are a few practices and what the research shows:


Acupuncture: Some trials suggest it can reduce menstrual pain associated with endometriosis. For example, one review found a “large effect” on menstrual pain for acupuncture versus usual care. But the evidence is still low certainty. PMC+2thedaohealth.com.au+2

Chinese herbal medicine (CHM): Some studies indicate benefits for symptom relief, shrinking lesions, improving fertility in endometriosis when used alongside or compared to Western treatments. PubMed+2BioMed Central+2

Combined therapy: Some meta-analyses suggest TCM combined with Western hormonal treatments may improve outcomes and reduce side effects. Frontiers+1

Lifestyle/diet: While less studied in TCM context specifically for endo, many complementary-care sources recommend movement, avoiding “cold” foods, improving circulation, etc. Total Wellness Centre+1


What you might take from this


Viewing endometriosis not just as “hormones gone wrong” but also as “flow blocked, circulation poor, energy/qi stagnation, cold/damp in pelvis” might open up different self-care avenues.

Practices like acupuncture, certain herbs, encouraging pelvic circulation (heat, massage, movement) may complement conventional care.

Diet and lifestyle may play a role in reducing the “environment” that fosters stagnation or inflammation.



What this doesn’t mean — important caveats


Eastern/traditional practices are not magic cures. The evidence is promising in some areas but limited in many others. E.g., many TCM studies have methodological limitations. PMC+1

Any herbal remedy or supplement should be vetted thoroughly (interactions, safety, quality) and discussed with your healthcare provider.

Because endometriosis can seriously impact fertility, pain, and organs, it often does warrant conventional medical evaluation (imaging, specialist care) alongside complementary practices.

The fact that many conventional treatments focus on hormones or surgery is partly because endo’s mechanisms are fairly hormone- and structure-driven (tissue responding to estrogen, lesions forming, etc) — so what feels frustrating has a rationale. But that doesn’t mean that is the only approach.

“Eastern practices” should complement, not necessarily replace, conventional care unless overseen by knowledgeable providers.

## Response:
Great questions — the short version is: no, using tampons is not established as a cause of Endometriosis, though the mechanisms surrounding menstruation and how tissue moves are part of what scientists are studying. I’ll walk you through what the research says about tampons + “back-flow” (retrograde menstruation) and then I’ll send you that menu of eastern/integrative practices we talked about.



Tampons & “back‐flow” (retrograde menstruation) — what the evidence says


What is retrograde menstruation?


Retrograde menstruation means that some menstrual blood (and endometrial tissue) doesn’t flow out the vagina but instead flows backwards through the fallopian tubes into the pelvic cavity. Cleveland Clinic+1

This is considered one of the possible contributors to endometriosis because those shed endometrial cells might implant in places outside the uterus. Voluson Club+1

However: It’s quite common for retrograde menstruation to happen, and yet only a subset of people develop endometriosis — so retrograde menstruation alone probably is not the whole story. Voluson Club+1


What about tampons / menstrual products?


Some have hypothesised that tampons or menstrual cups might increase risk because if they somehow “block outflow” they might promote more retrograde flow. A 2024 narrative review discussed this idea. PubMed

BUT: That review concluded the evidence is inconclusive. It noted: “Products that may reduce menstrual flow from the endometrial cavity to the vagina (i.e. tampons and menstrual cups) could facilitate retrograde menstruation … the evidence is scarce.” PubMed

Importantly: A fact‐check by Associated Press states that tampons do not cause endometriosis. They quoted experts saying tampons absorb rather than block flow, and that research doesn’t show higher rates of endometriosis among tampon users. AP News

Also: Some older research pointed to tampon use as potentially protective, though this is older and less conclusive. YaleNews


My interpretation


It seems extremely unlikely (based on current science) that tampon use is a direct cause of endometriosis.

The idea that menstrual products could meaningfully increase retrograde flow (and thus risk) remains speculative, weakly supported.

If you’re worried, you could monitor how your menstrual flow feels, how comfortable you are with your product choice, and consider switching if you feel one product irritates or stresses you — but there’s no strong evidence you must avoid tampons for the sake of endo risk.

Also bear in mind that endometriosis likely involves many factors — genetics, immune status, hormonal milieu, inflammation, endometrial cell survival — so focusing only on “product X caused it” simplifies a complex picture.
