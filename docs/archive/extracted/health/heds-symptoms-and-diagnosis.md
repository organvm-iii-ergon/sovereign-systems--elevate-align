## Response:
This is a very good question—and one a lot of people with “mystery autoimmune” labels eventually arrive at.


I’ll break this down cleanly and then connect the dots.



What is hEDS?


hEDS = hypermobile Ehlers–Danlos syndrome


It’s a genetic connective tissue disorder where the body’s collagen (the protein that gives structure, elasticity, and strength) is functionally weak or disorganized.


Unlike autoimmune diseases:


The immune system is not attacking the body

The problem is structural, not inflammatory by origin

But it creates secondary inflammation because the body is constantly compensating


There is no single genetic blood test yet for hEDS (unlike other EDS subtypes), which is one major reason it’s missed.



Common hEDS Symptoms (many people don’t realize these are connected)


🦴 Musculoskeletal


Joint hypermobility (even subtle or “used to be flexible”)

Joint pain without clear injury

Frequent sprains, strains, subluxations, or “things going out”

Early joint degeneration or arthritis-like pain

Muscle tightness despite being flexible (muscles overwork to stabilize)


🧠 Nervous system / fatigue


Chronic fatigue

Brain fog

Headaches or migraines

Dizziness or POTS-like symptoms (standing intolerance, heart rate spikes)


🩸 Inflammatory / autoimmune-like


Joint swelling or stiffness

Morning pain similar to RA

Elevated inflammatory markers sometimes

Pain that moves or migrates


🫀 Other connective-tissue signs


Easy bruising

Slow healing

Soft or stretchy skin (not always extreme)

GI issues (reflux, IBS, motility problems)

Pelvic floor dysfunction

Hormonal sensitivity (cycles hit harder)


Many people—especially women—get told:

“You’re too young for this,”
“Your labs don’t fully match RA,”
“It’s fibromyalgia,”
“It’s anxiety,”
“It’s autoimmune but unspecified.”


Why hEDS Is Often Misdiagnosed as RA or an Autoimmune Disease


1. Symptoms overlap


RA, lupus, and autoimmune conditions cause:


Joint pain

Swelling

Fatigue

Systemic symptoms


hEDS causes the same outward symptoms, but the source is mechanical instability, not immune attack.


2. Secondary inflammation confuses doctors


When joints aren’t stable:


Micro-injuries happen constantly

Fascia and nerves get irritated

The body releases inflammatory cytokines


This can:


Raise CRP/ESR slightly

Mimic inflammatory arthritis

Respond partially to steroids or NSAIDs (masking the real cause)


3. Autoimmune tests can be inconclusive


RF negative

Anti-CCP negative

ANA sometimes borderline or positive (very common in the population)


So doctors label it:

“Seronegative RA”
“Undifferentiated connective tissue disease”
“Autoimmune spectrum disorder”

When the actual connective tissue disorder is structural.



The Fascia Connection (this is the key most people miss)











4


Fascia is the body’s connective tissue web


It wraps:


Muscles

Organs

Nerves

Blood vessels

Joints


Think of fascia as the body’s internal scaffolding + communication network.


In hEDS:


Collagen in fascia is too elastic or disorganized

Fascia loses tensional integrity

The body becomes unstable everywhere, not just joints


This leads to:


Chronic myofascial pain

Trigger points

Nerve compression

Poor proprioception (you don’t sense joint position accurately)

Compensatory muscle guarding (tight muscles around loose joints)


So people feel:

“I’m stiff AND flexible”
“My muscles are tight but my joints feel unstable”
“Massage helps briefly, then pain returns”

That’s classic fascial instability.



Why This Feels Autoimmune (but isn’t)


Fascia is:


Highly innervated (lots of nerves)

Highly immune-active

Responsive to stress hormones


When fascia is dysregulated:


The nervous system stays in fight-or-flight

Pain becomes amplified

Inflammation becomes chronic


This creates a feedback loop:
Structural instability → fascial irritation → nervous system dysregulation → inflammation → pain


No antibodies required.



Why Women Are Disproportionately Affected


Estrogen affects collagen elasticity

Hormonal shifts worsen symptoms

Women are more likely to be flexible and dismissed

hEDS often runs in families but looks different in each generation


Many women with hEDS are:


“Former dancers / athletes / yoga girls”

Told flexibility is a good thing

Only diagnosed after injury, illness, or burnout tips the system over



Key Difference: Autoimmune vs hEDS

Autoimmune
hEDS
Immune attack
Structural collagen disorder
Antibodies present
No definitive lab test
Inflammation is primary
Inflammation is secondary
Immunosuppressants help
Stability & fascia care help
Damage from immunity
Damage from instability


If this resonates…


The next steps usually involve:


Clinical hypermobility assessment (Beighton + history)

Ruling out inflammatory arthritis properly

Fascia-focused rehab (not generic PT)

Nervous system regulation

Stabilization before stretching

Hormone-aware care

## Response:
No need to apologize — these are exactly the right questions, and you’re thinking about this in the right order. I’ll go step-by-step and then slow down on the nervous system, stabilization, and hormones, because that’s where people with hEDS actually start to feel better.



1️⃣ “Genetic” — does that mean one of my parents had it?


Yes, but not always obviously.


hEDS is believed to be autosomal dominant, meaning:


Only one parent needs to carry it

Each child has ~50% chance of inheriting it


BUT many parents were never diagnosed because:


They were just called “flexible”

Or they had chronic pain labeled as “aging,” “arthritis,” or “bad back”

Or they compensated well until later in life


It can look different across generations:


Parent = flexible, injury-prone, migraines

Child = pain, fatigue, dysautonomia, “autoimmune mystery”


So yes — it’s inherited, but often unrecognized.



2️⃣ Can hEDS be tested for?


Short answer: not with a blood test (yet)


There is no confirmed genetic marker for hEDS currently

Other EDS subtypes do have genetic tests


So diagnosis is clinical, based on:


Joint hypermobility

Symptom history

Exclusion of other causes


This is why hEDS is often missed or dismissed.



3️⃣ Difference between EDS and hEDS


Think of EDS as an umbrella.

Type
Key difference
EDS (general)
Group of connective tissue disorders
hEDS
Hypermobile type — most common
Other EDS types
Vascular, classical, etc. (often more severe, genetically testable)

hEDS is:


The most common

The least genetically understood

The most often misdiagnosed


hEDS focuses on:


Joint instability

Fascia dysfunction

Nervous system involvement



4️⃣ What is POTS?


POTS = Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome


It’s a form of dysautonomia (autonomic nervous system dysfunction).


What happens:


When you stand up, blood doesn’t return efficiently

Heart rate jumps to compensate

Brain gets less blood → symptoms


Common symptoms:


Dizziness when standing

Heart racing

Fatigue

Brain fog

Exercise intolerance

Feeling “wired but tired”


Why it’s linked to hEDS:


Fascia + collagen support blood vessels

If vessels are too stretchy → poor blood return

Nervous system overcompensates


So POTS is structural + nervous system, not “just anxiety.”



5️⃣ Collagen vs Creatine — will either help?


🧬 Collagen


What it is: amino acids (glycine, proline) used to build connective tissue


Reality check (important):


Eating collagen does not directly replace faulty collagen

But it can support tissue repair and fascia hydration


Who it helps most:


Joint pain

Fascia stiffness

Skin, tendons, ligaments

Gut lining


Best way to take it:


With vitamin C

10–20g/day

Think support, not cure


You having collagen already = totally reasonable to try.



💪 Creatine


What it is: energy support for muscles (ATP)


Why it matters in hEDS:


Muscles have to work overtime to stabilize joints

Creatine improves muscular endurance and strength

Indirectly improves joint stability


Creatine ≠ collagen
They do different jobs and can actually complement each other.


👉 If forced to choose:


Collagen = fascia & connective tissue support

Creatine = muscular stability & fatigue reduction



6️⃣ The MOST IMPORTANT PART: How to actually approach care


This is where most people go wrong — and why PT or yoga alone can make things worse.



🧠 Nervous System Regulation (foundation)


hEDS bodies often live in low-grade fight-or-flight because instability = perceived threat.


This causes:


Pain amplification

Inflammation

Fatigue

Poor healing


What actually helps:


Slow, predictable input

Gentle vagal stimulation

Safety before strength


Examples:


Nasal breathing (especially long exhales)

Supine or side-lying breathing

Very gentle somatic work

Avoid pushing through pain signals


If the nervous system isn’t regulated, nothing else sticks.



🧱 Stabilization before stretching (this is critical)


People with hEDS are often told to stretch — this is usually a mistake.


Why stretching hurts:


You’re already too mobile

Fascia lacks tension

Stretching increases instability


What to do instead:


Isometric strengthening

Small-range controlled movements

Core, hips, shoulders first

Build joint awareness (proprioception)


Think:

“Hold and support” before “lengthen and release”

Once stability is present, gentle mobility becomes safe again.



🌙 Hormone-aware care (often ignored)


Hormones directly affect collagen.


Estrogen:


Increases collagen elasticity

Can worsen instability around ovulation


Progesterone:


Can increase laxity for some people

Affects nervous system calm


This is why symptoms often:


Fluctuate with cycles

Spike pre-period

Feel worse during stress or illness


Hormone-aware care means:


Adjusting intensity across the cycle

More stabilization during high-estrogen phases

More rest and regulation during luteal phase

Not gaslighting yourself when symptoms vary



Why antibiotics or illness can “activate” everything


You mentioned this earlier — this is a BIG clue.


Illness or antibiotics can:


Disrupt gut collagen signaling

Increase systemic inflammation

Overload the nervous system

Remove compensatory buffers


The body crosses a threshold → symptoms explode.


Not autoimmune.
Decompensation.

3️⃣ Learning safe stabilization patterns

5️⃣ Mapping symptoms to your cycle

## Response:
You’re describing a very classic hEDS / connective-tissue pattern, and the fact that your inflammation is consistently high with negative autoimmune panels is actually one of the biggest clues that this is not RA.


Let’s go point by point, then we’ll do the four things you said yes to in a clean, grounded way.



First — why your labs look like this

High inflammation, negative RA/autoimmune panels (twice)

This pattern usually means:


The immune system is reactive, not attacking

Inflammation is coming from tissue strain, micro-injury, and nervous system stress

Not antibody-driven disease


In hEDS:


Joints are unstable → constant micro-damage

Fascia is irritated → immune signaling stays elevated

Muscles overwork → inflammatory byproducts

Nervous system stays on → cytokines stay high


So labs show:


↑ CRP / ESR

Normal RF, anti-CCP, ANA or borderline

Doctors shrug and say “inflammatory but unclear”


That’s mechanical inflammation, not autoimmune.



Is C4 creatine?


No — totally different things.


🔹 C4 (lab)


A complement protein in the immune system

Part of inflammatory signaling

Can be elevated or depleted in autoimmune disease


🔹 Creatine (supplement)


Helps muscles regenerate energy (ATP)

Improves strength, endurance, stability

Does not affect immune complement pathways


So:


C4 ≠ creatine

Creatine will not worsen inflammation

It often reduces pain by improving muscular support



Is Pilates good — or not without stabilization first?


Pilates can be excellent OR harmful depending on how it’s done.


⚠️ When Pilates can be a problem


Classical or fast-paced Pilates

Emphasis on flexibility, long lever movements

Cueing like “pull in hard” or “flatten your back”

Large ranges of motion before control


This can:


Increase joint shear

Overstretch already lax fascia

Trigger pain flares


✅ When Pilates is actually ideal for hEDS


Clinical / rehab-style Pilates

Slow, controlled, small range

Isometric holds

Neutral spine emphasis

Focus on control over range


Rule of thumb:

If it burns gently and feels stabilizing = good
If it feels stretchy, shaky, or relieving then worse later = too much

If you do Pilates, think:
“stability Pilates,” not “fitness Pilates.”



How to decrease estrogen naturally (very important for hEDS)


We don’t want to crash estrogen — just improve clearance and balance.


🥦 Support estrogen detox (liver + gut)


Estrogen is cleared through the liver → bile → gut → poop
If that pathway is slow, estrogen recirculates.


Foods that help:


Cruciferous veggies (broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage)

Cooked > raw if gut is sensitive

Carrots (bind estrogen in the gut)

Flaxseed (1–2 tbsp ground)


🍵 Teas that help


Spearmint (gentle anti-androgen, hormone calming)

Dandelion root (liver support)

Nettle (mineral support + inflammation reduction)

Ginger (reduces estrogen-driven inflammation)


💩 Gut matters more than supplements


Daily bowel movement is critical

Constipation = estrogen recycling


⚠️ What to avoid (or reduce)


Excess alcohol

Ultra-low calorie dieting

Overtraining (raises estrogen via stress)


This is balancing, not suppressing.



Now let’s do the FOUR things you asked for 👇



1️⃣ Self-screen: hEDS vs RA (at home)


Ask yourself honestly:


Did you used to be very flexible as a kid or teen?

Do you feel both tight and unstable?

Do joints hurt without visible damage?

Do symptoms migrate?

Do flares follow stress, illness, hormones?

Does rest help more than meds?


RA usually has:


Symmetric joint damage

Progressive erosion

Morning stiffness > 1 hour consistently

Clear imaging changes


hEDS usually has:


Variable pain

Normal imaging early

“My body doesn’t feel held together”



2️⃣ Nervous system regulation (daily baseline)


This is not optional — it’s foundational.


Simple daily practices:


Nasal breathing only

Longer exhales than inhales (4 in / 6 out)

Supine or side-lying breathing

Gentle humming or sighing


Avoid:


Breath holds

Intense breathwork

Cold plunges (early on)


Goal:

Teach your body it is safe enough to heal


3️⃣ Stabilization before stretching (how to train)


What to do:


Isometric holds (no movement)

Small-range control

Closed-chain movements

Slow tempo


Examples:


Wall sits

Dead bug holds (small range)

Glute bridges with holds

Shoulder blade setting

Core engagement without crunching


Pain rule:


Mild effort = okay

Sharp pain or delayed flare = stop


Stretching comes later, gently, after strength.



4️⃣ Hormone-aware rhythm (this matters)


High estrogen phases (follicular / ovulation):


More joint laxity

Do less range

More stabilization

Shorter workouts


Luteal / pre-period:


More nervous system care

Lower intensity

More rest and warmth


If symptoms spike cyclically → this confirms the pattern.



Supplements — where collagen & creatine fit


Collagen: supports fascia & connective tissue

Creatine: supports muscles doing stabilization


They are supportive tools, not fixes — but often helpful.
