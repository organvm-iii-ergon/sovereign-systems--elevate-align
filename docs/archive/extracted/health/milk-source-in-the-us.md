## Response:
That claim — that about 87% of U.S. beef is imported from Saudi Arabia or elsewhere — is not accurate, based on the best available data.


Here’s a breakdown of what the actual import/export picture looks like for beef in the United States, and the key things to know:



✅ What the data shows


The U.S. is primarily a beef producer, not a net importer. The United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) publishes trade-data tables for “beef and veal: monthly U.S. trade” showing imports and exports by country. Southern Ag Today+3Economic Research Service+3AMS+3

According to a recent overview of U.S. beef imports by country, the top sources of imported beef into the U.S. are:
Canada (~ 42.6% of import value) US Import Data

Mexico (~ 28.5%) US Import Data

Australia (~ 19.3%) US Import Data

In 2024 the U.S. imported 4.64 billion pounds of beef, which is a small fraction compared to total domestic beef production. US Import Data+2Southern Ag Today+2

Some media sources say that “imports of beef from countries including Australia, Canada and Brazil … account for about 12% of the total consumed in the U.S.” AP News



❌ What the 87% figure misses / why it’s off


The 87% figure would imply that only ~13% of all U.S.-consumed beef is domestically produced, which is almost certainly false.

The major import sources are Canada, Mexico, Australia, Brazil, etc. Saudi Arabia does not appear in the major lists for supplying beef to the U.S.

Also, there is a difference between “imports of beef” and “total consumption of beef”. Even if imports are rising, domestic production remains by far the bulk of the supply for U.S. consumers.

Some labeling/terminology confusion: Meat can come from cattle born or raised overseas, or from overseas slices/trimmings, but still be processed or packaged in the U.S., which sometimes confuses reporting or what consumers interpret.



🔍 Key take-aways for you


If you buy beef in the U.S., there’s a very high probability it was produced in the U.S. (or at least largely domestic). Imported beef does make a meaningful contribution — especially for certain cuts or lean trimmings — but it’s not the vast majority.

In recent years, imports are increasing, because domestic cattle herd numbers have fallen, feed costs are up, droughts impact production, etc. Southern Ag Today+1

If you see a steak with a label like “Product of USA,” there are nuanced rules about what that exactly means (for example: born, raised, slaughtered, processed all in the U.S.). AP News

If you’re sourcing beef and want to know whether it’s domestic vs imported, you can check for country-of-origin labels (depending on the store/brand) or ask the butcher / supplier.

## Response:
Here are two components of your question — the trade figures for U.S. beef, and the welfare / slaughterhouse concerns — followed by what that means for you as a consumer.



1. U.S. beef production & imports


✅ Production


According to the United States Department of Agriculture’s Economic Research Service (ERS), the U.S. beef sector had “total beef disappearance” (which is roughly consumption) of about 26–28 billion pounds in recent years. Economic Research Service+2Economic Research Service+2

For example, one chart notes that in 2022 U.S. beef production reached a peak of about 28.4 billion lb, and it is projected to drop to about 24.8 billion lb in 2027 before climbing again. Economic Research Service+1


🔍 Imports


In 2024, U.S. beef imports hit a record of about 4.635 billion pounds. Economic Research Service+1

The major supplying countries for beef imports in recent data (2024) were:
Australia — about 24% of U.S. beef imports. Economic Research Service+1

Canada — about 22% of imports. Economic Research Service

Brazil — about 15% of imports. Economic Research Service+1

Mexico — about 13% of imports. Economic Research Service

New Zealand — about 12% of imports. Economic Research Service


📊 What it implies


If U.S. production is ~26-28 billion lb and imports are ~4.6 billion lb, then imports represent roughly 15–20% of the domestic consumption or production level — not anywhere near 80-90%.

Note: “consumption” and “production” aren’t exactly the same (there are exports, stock changes, etc.), but the scale is clear: domestic production is the bulk.

Also, interestingly, projections show that when U.S. production drops, imports tend to go up — and when production increases, imports tend to retreat. Economic Research Service+1


So the claim that “87% of U.S. beef is imported” is not supported by these data.



2. Slaughterhouses & animal welfare concerns


You also mentioned that even large slaughterhouses in the U.S. are “awful and inhumane.” There’s substantial evidence to support that many have serious welfare and labour issues.


🔴 Animal welfare / industry structure


Many animals in the U.S. are raised in industrialised systems (sometimes called Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations, or CAFOs). These systems are critiqued for serious welfare issues — lack of space, inability for animals to carry out natural behaviours, crowded conditions, and so on. Animal Welfare Institute+1

For example: “The vast majority of farmed animals are raised in conventional, industrial agriculture systems… These systems are designed to maximise productivity and profit … but they create serious welfare problems for the animals.” Animal Welfare Institute

Regarding slaughterhouses: Even though they are among the more-regulated parts of the livestock industry, oversight is still incomplete. A peer-reviewed study notes that while slaughter is heavily regulated (in food safety etc.), “as for workers, the environment, and animals, there are some laws … however these laws are both inadequate and poorly enforced.” PMC


🧭 Labeling & transparency issues


For example, the labeling “Product of USA” or “Made in USA” has sometimes meant only that the animal was processed in the U.S., not necessarily born, raised, and slaughtered here. AP News

Welfare certification programs exist (e.g., American Humane Association’s “American Humane Certified”) but have limitations and critics. Wikipedia



3. What this all means for you


Origin: If you buy beef in the U.S., it’s very likely it was produced domestically (born, raised, slaughtered here) though there are significant imports (≈ 15–20 % of volume).

Welfare / ethics: If the treatment of animals and the conditions of slaughter concern you, you’ll want to look for additional assurance beyond just “U.S. origin.” Labels, certifications, farm-transparency, smaller-scale farms, etc. will matter.

What to look for:
Country of origin labelling: While not perfect, trying to see whether the full chain (birth → raising → slaughtering) happened in the U.S. is useful.

Welfare certifications or brands that explicitly commit to better animal treatment.

Smaller, local producers or farms that you can visit, or whose practices you can research.

Trade vs local support: Some people prefer buying from domestic producers for reasons of traceability, supporting local farms, or minimizing long-haul logistics. The data show domestic production still dominates, so you can choose U.S.-produced beef without sacrificing availability.

Disclosure & demand: Consumer demand for higher welfare, better transparency, and more humane practices is growing. The industry is under pressure to improve.
