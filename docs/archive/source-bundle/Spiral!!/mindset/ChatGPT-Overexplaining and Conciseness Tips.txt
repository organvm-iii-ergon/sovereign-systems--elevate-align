## Prompt:
Where does the need to over explain come from and how can I work on my posture & being more concise when I speak

## Response:
Ah, yes—overexplaining is such a common pattern, and it usually comes from a mix of internal drives and external conditioning. Let’s break it down carefully:



Where the need to over explain comes from


Fear of being misunderstood or judged
You might overexplain to make sure people “get it” or to prevent criticism.

Example: You give extra context because you assume the other person won’t understand your intent otherwise.

Perfectionism or wanting to be ‘right’
Overexplaining often comes from trying to control the narrative or cover every angle so nothing can be questioned.

People-pleasing tendencies
Sometimes you feel responsible for how others perceive your thoughts. You overexplain to be helpful, to sound competent, or to avoid conflict.

Past experiences
If you’ve been corrected, ignored, or misunderstood in the past, your brain might have learned: “Better to give all the details, just in case.”

Anxious energy in communication
Overexplaining is often more about internal tension than the listener’s actual need. It’s a way of reassuring yourself that you’re being clear or acceptable.



How to speak more concisely & improve posture


Anchor your body first
Stand or sit tall, shoulders back, chest open.

Posture physically helps your voice carry and your mind feel grounded. When your body is open, you’re less likely to fill the space with extra words.

Pause before speaking
Count 2-3 seconds after someone asks a question or you form a thought.

Pauses give your brain a chance to prioritize the key points instead of flooding with details.

Identify your key message
Before speaking, ask: “What is the one thing I want them to remember?”

Start with that, then add details only if needed.

Practice “edit in real-time”
When speaking, imagine each sentence costs energy. Ask yourself: “Does this add value?”

Train yourself to notice filler phrases, tangents, or repeating points.

Use confident language
Instead of hedging (“I think maybe, um…”), state: “Here’s what happened…”

Confidence naturally reduces the need to overjustify.

Body alignment cues
Feet grounded, spine straight, hands relaxed but expressive.

When your posture is confident, your brain feels more secure and you naturally speak more efficiently.

Micro-practice
Pick a short story or explanation and record yourself.

Edit it down to half the words without losing meaning. Practice delivering that version until it feels natural.



💡 Extra tip: Overexplaining is often a signal you’re overthinking internally. If you combine grounding your body + clarifying your key message + pausing, you’ll notice the overexplaining slowly melts away.

Your 5-Minute Confidence + Clarity Drill


1. Posture Reset (1 minute)


This is your foundation—everything builds from here.


Do this:

“I don’t need to prove. I just need to express.”

💡 This immediately reduces the urge to overcompensate with words.



2. The “One-Line Power” Exercise (2 minutes)


This trains your brain to lead with clarity instead of explanation.


Pick a simple topic (what you did today, your business, an idea)


Round 1:
Explain it however you normally would (don’t filter)


Round 2:
Now say the same thing in ONE sentence


Round 3 (most important):
Say it again in one sentence—but slower, grounded, and confident


Example:


Long version: “So today I was working on this funnel and kind of trying to figure out the messaging…”

One-line:“Today I refined my funnel messaging to improve conversions.”


🔥 This is how you train precision thinking



3. Pause + Hold Drill (1 minute)


This breaks the habit of rushing or filling space.


Do this:


Say a sentence out loud

Then pause for 2 seconds (even if it feels awkward)

Maintain eye contact (or imagine it)


Example:

“That’s the direction I’m moving in.”
(pause… breathe… hold presence)

💡 This is where confidence is felt, not spoken.


Most people overexplain to avoid that silence—this trains you to own it.



4. Cut It in Half Game (1 minute)


This rewires your brain to naturally edit.


Take any sentence and shorten it by 50%


Examples:


“I just wanted to kind of check in and see if maybe this would work for you”
→“Does this work for you?”

“I feel like what I’m trying to say is…”
→“My point is…”


🔥 This is the fastest way to eliminate filler and sound powerful.
