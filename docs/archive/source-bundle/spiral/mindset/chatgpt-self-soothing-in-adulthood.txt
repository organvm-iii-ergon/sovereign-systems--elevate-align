## Prompt:
Can you tell as an adult if your parents let you “self soothe” as a child

## Response:
While it can be challenging to know for sure if your parents let you "self-soothe" as a child, certain behavioral patterns and emotional tendencies in adulthood might provide clues. Self-soothing refers to a child's ability to calm themselves without external comfort, often fostered by parenting styles during infancy and childhood.


Potential Indicators in Adulthood:


Attachment Style:
If you have an avoidant attachment style, it might suggest you were left to self-soothe often, leading you to prioritize independence over seeking comfort from others.

Conversely, an anxious attachment style could indicate inconsistent caregiving, where you sometimes had to self-soothe but also received comfort sporadically.

Emotional Regulation:
Struggling to manage intense emotions or relying heavily on others for comfort could suggest you were not taught or encouraged to self-soothe effectively.

Alternatively, being overly self-reliant and avoiding emotional vulnerability might reflect being left to cope alone frequently as a child.

Interpersonal Relationships:
Difficulty trusting others or forming close relationships might stem from a childhood where emotional needs weren't consistently met.

Fear of abandonment or rejection can also suggest a lack of early emotional support.

Self-Compassion and Coping Mechanisms:
Adults who struggle to practice self-compassion or rely on unhealthy coping mechanisms (e.g., numbing emotions, overworking) may not have learned healthy self-soothing techniques in childhood.

Memories or Stories from Caregivers:
Reflecting on how your parents describe their parenting style or specific memories they share can give insight. For example, if they mention letting you "cry it out," it might indicate a tendency toward encouraging self-soothing.


How to Reflect:


Journaling: Write about your earliest memories of comfort or distress. Do you recall being comforted by a caregiver or figuring things out on your own?

Ask Your Parents: If possible, ask them directly about their parenting approach during your infancy.

Therapy: A therapist can help you explore patterns in your relationships and emotions that might be connected to early childhood experiences.


Even if you discover patterns linked to your early experiences, it's important to remember that your brain remains adaptable. You can learn new, healthy ways to soothe yourself and relate to others as an adult.
